Everyone talks about heartbreak.

About losing people.
About failing exams.
About getting rejected.
About not having enough money.

Those are painful. No doubt.

But I don't think they're the hardest part of being human.

I think the hardest part is waking up with questions that don't have answers.

Questions like:

Who am I?

What am I really supposed to do?

Am I building the right life?

Why does everything that once excited me suddenly feel distant?

The strange thing is that these questions don't disappear because you're busy.

You attend classes.
You go to work.
You finish deadlines.
You meet people.
You laugh.
You stay productive.

From the outside, everything looks normal.

But late at night, when the world becomes quiet, those questions return as if they had only been waiting for everyone else to leave.

And in the morning, they are still there.

It's almost unfair.

You spend hours thinking, hoping that today you'll finally understand something. Instead, you wake up with the same uncertainty you carried to bed.

It becomes a loop.

Not because you're lazy.
Not because you're doing nothing.
But because some questions simply don't reward effort with immediate answers.

Maybe that's the most uncool part of being human.

We are surrounded by people chasing something.

A promotion.
A startup.
A relationship.
A dream university.
A better salary.

Everyone seems to know what they're running toward.

Then there's you.

Walking.
Looking around.
Wondering if you're even on the right road.

That comparison slowly becomes exhausting.

For most of my life, I was driven by becoming someone better.

I wanted to learn everything I could.
I explored things I never imagined myself doing.
I believed growth was the answer.

Not because someone forced me to.

Because I didn't want to inherit a life that was limited by fear or circumstance. I wanted to build something different from what the generations before me could.

That dream kept me moving.

Until one day, it didn't.

Nothing dramatic happened.

The drive simply became quieter.

And that's confusing.

How do you explain losing something that once defined you?

Maybe this is what people call an existential crisis.

Maybe it isn't.

I don't know.

All I know is that the hardest battles aren't always visible.

Sometimes you're fighting a version of yourself that no one else can see.

Sometimes you're trying to rediscover a reason to wake up excited again.

And sometimes, the bravest thing you can do is continue moving even when certainty never arrives.

I'm beginning to think that life isn't about finding one perfect answer.

Maybe it's about becoming someone who can live with unanswered questions without letting them steal tomorrow.

I don't have the answers yet.

Maybe I never will.

But if you've ever gone to bed carrying invisible questions and woken up with them still sitting beside you, know this:

You're probably not as alone as you think.

Maybe this is just one of those strangely human experiences that almost nobody talks about.

And maybe that's exactly why we should.
